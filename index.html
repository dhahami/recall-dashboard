01
The Research Question

Finding the Signal in Recall Risk

U.S. auto recalls have grown in frequency and regulatory scrutiny over 25 years, evolving from mechanical failures to complex software-driven events. Yet markets don't react to every recall equally — and identifying when they do creates a tradeable edge.

The National Highway Traffic Safety Administration publicly records every U.S. vehicle recall. Each filing describes the defect, the consequence, the corrective action, and the population of vehicles at risk. Over 25 years, this database has grown into one of the most comprehensive records of automotive product risk available to investors.

Most analyses stop at recall volume. This study goes further — building a severity scoring model using keyword-based consequence matching, a mechanical vs. technical classifier powered by Claude Sonnet, and a surprise metric that measures how unexpected a recall is relative to a firm's own history. The result is a systematic framework for identifying which recalls actually move markets.

Using a Bai-Perron breakpoint test, we identify two structural regime shifts: 2014 (the Takata airbag crisis, triggering a wave of regulation) and 2021 (the onset of the EV/tech era). Market reactions to recalls differ dramatically across these three regimes — and so does the optimal trading strategy.

The central finding: the edge lies in how markets differentially reprice recall risk, not in the recall itself. Markets do not adjust immediately. The strongest signal appears only when recall surprise aligns with firm sensitivity — a combination that produces statistically significant abnormal returns in the [−1,+7] window post-announcement.

The Monaco Method

Firm Sensitivity Model

Not all firms react to recalls the same way. The Firm Sensitivity Model classifies manufacturers into three signal types — based on whether their average CAR is significantly positive, negative, or neutral — across three market eras.

Positive Signal
Views recalls as proactive management

Risk decreases → confidence premiums. Market interprets recall announcements as evidence the firm monitors quality actively.

Negative Signal
Raises quality & cost concerns

Risk increases → negative returns. Recalls signal operational problems or regulatory liability that investors price in immediately.

Risk Priced In
No new information to the market

No reaction. Recall risk is already embedded in the stock price — announcements carry no incremental information.

The Surprise Metric

Signal vs. Noise

Surprise Score = Actual Risk − Expected Risk. Built from each firm's historical recall history, severity, and affected vehicles — it measures how unexpected a recall is relative to a firm's own baseline.

Formula
Surprise = Actual Risk − Expected Risk
Where Actual Risk = Severity Score × log(Affected Vehicles), and Expected Risk is derived from the firm's trailing 365-day recall history.

Appendix

Extended methodology, model parameters, and statistical detail not shown on the competition poster. Intended for judges and reviewers who want to examine the full analytical framework.

A1 — Text Analysis: Severity Keyword Scoring & Mechanical vs. Technical Classification
Text analysis covered two distinct tasks: (1) keyword-based severity scoring — assigning consequence weights using a tiered dictionary of danger terms anchored to DOT/HLDI liability benchmarks; and (2) LLM classification via Claude Sonnet — distinguishing mechanical from technical root causes using full consequence text. Both pipelines produced auditable output columns added to the final dataset.
1
Severity Scoring via Keyword Matching
Each recall's consequence summary was scanned for danger-tier keywords. Presence of a keyword added that tier's weight to the recall's severity score. Scores were then banded into Low (0.0–3.0), Moderate (3.0–5.9), and High (6.0–10.0).
# Tier weights applied if keyword found in consequence text
TIER_WEIGHTS = {
  'death': 40, 'fatal': 40,
  'fire': 30, 'explosion': 25,
  'rollover': 25, 'crash': 20,
  'injury': 15, 'loss of function': 10,
  'property': 5
}
2
Text Preprocessing & Combination
The recall description, consequence summary, and corrective action were combined into a single combined_text field per recall — lowercased and stripped of punctuation — for use in the mechanical vs. technical classification step.
df['combined_text'] = (
  df['description_clean'] + ' ' +
  df['consequence_clean']
).str.lower().str.replace(r'[^\w\s]', '')
3
Rule-Based Keyword Scoring for Mech/Tech
Two scores — mech_score and tech_score — were computed from keyword dictionaries. Mechanical keywords included terms like "brake," "crack," "weld," "leak." Technical keywords included "software," "firmware," "sensor," "module," "ECU," "algorithm."
brake · weld · leak · crack · seal software · firmware · sensor · ECU · module ambiguous · unknown · other
4
LLM Classification via Claude Sonnet
Every recall was sent to Claude Sonnet with a strict system prompt defining root-cause criteria. The model returned: a label (MECHANICAL / TECHNICAL / AMBIGUOUS), a confidence score (0–1), and a one-sentence rationale.

5
Output: Four Auditable Columns
The final dataset added four columns per recall, making every classification auditable: recall_class_v2, class_confidence, class_rationale, and class_method (LLM vs. rule-based fallback).
MECHANICAL · 4,264 recalls · 78.4% TECHNICAL · 1,085 recalls · 19.9% AMBIGUOUS · 87 recalls · 1.6%
Why LLM Classification?

Keyword scoring misclassifies borderline cases — e.g., an electrical recall caused by a corroded wire (mechanical root cause) vs. a software glitch (technical). Claude Sonnet reads the full consequence text and applies root-cause reasoning, not surface-level term matching.

Classification Example
// Recall text (simplified)
"The positive battery cable connection may become loose, causing stalling and increasing the risk of fire."
// LLM output
label: MECHANICAL
confidence: 0.97
rationale: "Root cause is a physically loose/improperly secured cable connection, requiring mechanical inspection and repair."

Sharpe Formula
S = (Rp − Rf) / σp
Where Rp = strategy return, Rf = risk-free rate, σp = annualized standard deviation of returns. A higher Sharpe indicates more return per unit of risk.

Affected Weight Formula
Severity bands are adjusted by an affected weight (log-scaled vehicle count) to produce a final composite risk score. This prevents large-volume minor recalls from outscoring small-volume critical ones on raw count alone.
actual_risk = severity_score × log(n_vehicles)
expected_risk = f(prior_365d_history)
surprise = actual_risk − expected_risk

A5 — Limitations & Future Work
Acknowledged constraints on the current study and directions for extension.
Transaction CostsBid-ask spreads and brokerage fees are not reflected in the backtest. Real-world implementation would reduce gross win rates and P&L figures.
Pre-Announcement LeakageLimited public information is available before the official NHTSA filing date. Any pre-filing media coverage would affect the entry signal timing.
API Rate LimitsLLM classification was subject to Anthropic API rate limits, requiring batched processing. A small number of recalls were classified via fallback rule-based method.
Firm CoverageDeep firm-level breakdowns (Pre/Post/Tech era) fully completed for Ford and Tesla. Other firms remain at pooled post-2014 level in this version.
Short-Selling ConstraintsNot all stocks in the dataset have liquid options or easy short access. Practical execution of the SHORT strategy may require ETF or options-based implementation.
Future WorkExtend firm-level analysis to all major manufacturers, incorporate intraday timing signals, and test the framework on European recall databases (RAPEX).
